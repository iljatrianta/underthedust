From the end of the 19th CENTURY , the growth of the mining sector attracted many people to South Limburg. There was a great demand for work, and mining provided it.

1965

The Dutch government announced the closure of all coal mines in South Limburg.

The discovery of natural gas, cheap coal from abroad, and changing energy sources.

Mines became economically unsustainable.

Around 1970, shortly after the mine closures, a clear drug problem became visible in South Limburg.

Due to massive unemployment, a lack of future prospects, and the collapse of social structures, especially young people and former miners became vulnerable.

Soft and hard drugs emerged as an escape from boredom, frustration, and mental health issues.

ADDICTION

Spread quickly in some neighborhoods, making the social consequences of the mine closures felt for many years.

1974

Mine Oranje-Nassau I in Heerlen closed its doors as the very last coal mine in the Netherlands.

The end of a

70

year era

The closure marked not only the end of mining, but also the definitive beginning of an uncertain future for the region and its residents.

Entire neighborhoods lost their economic foundation.

Families were left without work, income, and future prospects.

The economic decline created tensions in neighborhoods.

The result was growing social problems and a deteriorating living environment.

1980

In the 1980s, the drug problem in South Limburg increased sharply.

Persistent unemployment, poverty, and hopelessness made urban areas especially vulnerable.

Hard drugs became increasingly visible in public spaces.

Illegal trade and addiction grew, partly due to the lack of jobs and guidance.

Prostitution also increased during this period. For some residents, it became a way to survive in a region where regular work was scarce.

In recent years, Heerlen has actively repositioned itself to strengthen the city's image. Culture plays a major role in this.

Parkstad Limburg Theaters serves as a cultural anchor and attracts visitors from both inside and outside the region.

1991

The international festival Cultura Nova also puts Heerlen on the map every year as a city of performing arts and experimentation.

2008

Youth and urban culture also get room to grow.

One example is the Street Dance Festival, which shows that Heerlen has not only a past, but also a contemporary and creative future.

2019

The station and the Maankwartier district were renewed to reconnect Heerlen with itself.

For years, the railway formed a physical and social divide in the city.

With the opening of the new station and Maankwartier in 2019, that barrier was broken and space emerged for meeting, housing, culture, and future-oriented urban development.

2025

In 2025, the Dutch national government introduced a new compensation scheme for mining damage in Limburg. The scheme was developed together with the province and involved municipalities.

Many homes in South Limburg suffer from cracks, subsidence, and structural problems caused by former mining in the region.

From late 2025 / early 2026, private homeowners can report mining damage via the Institute for Environment, People and Mining Limburg.

They can apply for compensation or repair, usually up to around €10,000, with higher amounts for severe damage.

Fifty years after the closure of Limburg's coal mines, residents can finally receive compensation for mining damage.

2026

Heerlen's image is still strongly shaped by socio-economic indicators.

Despite investments in renewal, a persistent image of disadvantage remains, rooted in the aftermath of the mine closures. This image is therefore not just a feeling, but also based on measurable data and policy.
